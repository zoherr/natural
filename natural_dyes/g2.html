Altering pH in a Dye Bath


Adjusting the pH of the dye is most effective towards the end of the dyeing process. Introduce either an acid or alkali into the dye bath and allow the fabric or yarn to remain until a color change occurs.


The grey fabric swatches in the photo opposite were rinsed in tap water, changing colors from pink/purple to grey/blue. Hibiscus flowers and mahonia berries are naturally acidic (orange pH strip), while tap water is closer to neutral (green pH strip). After dyeing, rinsing excess dye, the fabric can be rinsed in water with a splash of vinegar to restore the purple color.


If you induce a color change by altering the pH, be aware that washing the fabric or yarn in water of a different pH may cause colors to revert. To maintain the desired color, consider adding vinegar or bicarbonate of soda to the water during washing.


Leftover Dye


If you believe the dye bath is not exhausted (i.e., enough dye particles remain to dye more fabric), add more fabric or yarn to the dye pot. It may result in a lighter shade, but it's a way to avoid wasting precious dye.


The second dye bath often produces different colors as many plants contain multiple pigments. For example, avocado skin produces pinks in the first dye bath and then often browner shades of pink thereafter.


To store dye for future use, simmer away most of the water to leave a concentrated dye. Once cooled, pour it into an airtight glass jar and store in the fridge for several weeks. When ready to use, pour it back into the dye pot and add as much water as necessary to cover the fibers. Note that vigorous boiling can dull the color, and fresh dye often yields the best colors.


Solar Dyeing


Solar dyeing is the ultimate slow dyeing method, producing subtly different colors and tones compared to standard immersion dyeing. Place soya milk-mordanted fabric or yarn in a glass jar filled with dye and leave it on a sunny windowsill for a few weeks. The longer the jar is left, the darker the fibers will (hopefully) dye. Regularly check for mold growth and wash thoroughly at the end of the process.
